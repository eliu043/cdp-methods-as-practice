A.I. Toys

Ani Liu's algorithmically generated toy series turns gendered consumer data into evidence. The work asks what happens when a machine learns from toys already sorted into "girls" and "boys."

Artist page Digital series Unboxing Mania
central_argument.txt

AI does not invent gender stereotypes from nowhere. It learns them from the culture we feed it.

A.I. Toys uses machine learning to transform real toy-market data into absurd new toy concepts. The generated toys are funny because they are recognizable: their logic comes from actual product categories, marketing language, and social expectations.

Girls dataset

Jewelry / domestic chores / dolls / animals

Pink appears here as a quoted consumer signal: not an endorsement, but a marker of the narrow roles repeatedly attached to toys marketed toward girls.

Boys dataset

Weaponry / electronics / cars / construction

Blue appears as the parallel quoted signal, pointing to how power, machines, building, and conflict are packaged as masculine play.

artist_words.html

Gender is constructed through objects of play.

The toys described on Liu's screens were generated by a machine learning algorithm trained on real online consumer products labeled as "girls" and "boys" toys. Liu frames the resulting objects as reflections of the values adults place on children through design, marketing, and play.

Source: Liu, Ani. "A.I. Toys (MEGA HAUL RANDOM and MIXED SURPRISE ULTRA RARE UNBOXING COMPILATION!!!)." Ani Liu, 2022.

visual_aesthetic_representation.txt

Visual and aesthetic representation

Toy aesthetics

Liu adopts the aesthetics of toys, packaging, the retail shelf, and digital displays to immediately convey familiarity around the act of purchasing and unboxing toys. That familiarity is reinforced through bright colors and plastic materiality, then destabilized when the viewer sees the exaggerated blobby forms.

Contemporaries

Liu's work can be situated within a community of artists working at the intersection of A.I., feminism, media, and speculation. Close contemporaries include Lauren Lee McCarthy, with her investigations of intimacy and surrogacy, as well as Kate Crawford and Trevor Paglen's work on datasets and machine learning.

contextual_analysis.txt

Contextual analysis

2021-2022

Machine learning enters the art field

A.I. Toys was developed around 2021-2022, a recent moment but also one near the beginning of increased machine-learning use in art production. The work also arrives a few years after Target's initiative to stop separating "girls' toys" and "boys' toys" in its stores.

Parenthood

Gendered toys become personal evidence

From a personal standpoint, Liu has stated that the project grew out of becoming a parent and noticing the gendered nature of toys she was purchasing for her daughter.

Machine bias

Algorithms are not neutral

The work also builds on, and comments on, machine bias explored by journalists like Julia Angwin. By the mid-2010s, the idea that algorithms can reproduce racial, gender, economic, and other forms of bias had already been established and had begun moving into art, design, and media circles.

Ecologies of Care

Care work and childcare objects

The project is nested within Liu's Ecologies of Care exhibition, which examined postpartum experiences, the labor of motherhood, and the material cultures of childcare. The exhibition included programmed feeding tubes, transparent sculptures of a pig's uterus encasing human fetuses, and drawings depicting digital screen times.

ontological_analysis.csv

Ontological analysis

This table treats the project as a set of related entities: data sources, categories, models, objects, displays, and audiences that together produce the work's critique of gendered toy culture.

Component Description How it functions in the project
Online toy listings Existing retail products sold through platforms such as Amazon and Target The source material from which the project draws its data.
Gender categories "Girls' toys" and "boys' toys" Binary structure that the project critiques.
Product descriptions Marketing language, titles, tags, descriptors The text that the machine learning algorithm learns from.
Dataset Scraped toy data organized by gendered category Data to test the hypothesis that toys are gendered.
Machine-learning model A computational system trained on the toy data It extracts patterns and generates speculative new toys based on existing cultural stereotypes.
3D printed toys Physical toy prototypes Plastic toys that illustrate bias.
Screens / digital displays Interface for displaying generated toys They connect the project back to online retail and digital consumption.
Packaging Commodity aesthetics Allows the work to resemble a toy product or collectible, not just an art object.
Children Implied users of the toys They do not participate directly in the work, but Liu draws from learnings and experiences from her own children.
Parents Consumers and mediators Parents purchase gendered toys, often through inherited assumptions.
Retail platforms Amazon, Target, and related e-commerce systems They categorize, recommend, and normalize gendered product distinctions.
The viewer Gallery audience The viewer completes the work by recognizing the absurdity of the generated outputs.

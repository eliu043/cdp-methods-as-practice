Speculations on Shared Suburban Life

A growing archive about two forms of speculative development: subdivisions built for future residents and data centers built for future computation.

Work in progress · Summer 2026

01 Research question(s)What questions does the project raise? What social, technical, environmental, design, or policy problems are at stake?

North Texas is building two suburbs. One is composed of houses intended for future residents. The other is composed of data centers intended for future computation.

Both convert forecasts of demand into present-day claims on land, electricity, water, roads, and public finance. This project investigates how those claims become spatially and politically actionable.

How are forecasts of residential and computational demand used to allocate North Texas land and infrastructure, and who gains or loses when speculative futures become present-day commitments?

Population projections, electrical-load requests, site-selection models, zoning maps, tax incentives, and infrastructure plans do not merely predict regional growth. They help produce it.

An earlier thesis on Zillow examined how estimates, return-on-investment calculations, and market trends shape housing. This project asks how land is computationally and institutionally prepared to become a market product.

10Rhetorical argumentIf design is an argument for something, what does this project want to say?

North Texas growth is often represented as the natural consequence of population and technological demand. This project argues that growth becomes actionable only after institutions translate it into forecasts, zoning categories, infrastructure plans, financial incentives, and suitable sites.

The subdivision and the data-center campus are two architectures of anticipated demand. One materializes forecasts of future residents; the other materializes forecasts of future computation. Both transform land in the present on behalf of futures that have not yet arrived.

The project argues that these futures are not inevitable. They are governed through protocols that can be examined, contested, and potentially rewritten.

11Potential capstoneGesture toward a project form without proposing a solution before the problem is understood.

The capstone may become an interactive and performable atlas of the North Texas development frontier.

At the regional scale, it would trace several decades of land conversion, residential construction, data-center development, and infrastructure expansion across Dallas–Fort Worth.

At the municipal scale, it would examine Red Oak as a place where agricultural land, residential growth, economic-development policy, and hyperscale computation meet.

At the parcel scale, it would reconstruct the institutional and computational process through which selected landscapes became sites for houses, servers, substations, roads, and future investment.

The atlas would not produce a single verdict about where development should occur. Instead, it would allow users to encounter several incompatible definitions of suitability.

A covenant-based interface would act as a form of friction. It might require the user to confront testimony, ecological duration, water obligations, maintenance, or uncertainty before proceeding through the model. In some situations, it may prevent calculation altogether.

The project would ask whether a map can operate less like a development instrument and more like a space of negotiation.

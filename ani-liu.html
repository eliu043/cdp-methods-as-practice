A.I. Toys

Ani Liu's algorithmically generated toy series turns gendered consumer data into evidence. The work asks what happens when a machine learns from toys already sorted into "girls" and "boys."

Artist page Digital series Unboxing Mania
01Argument

AI does not invent gender stereotypes from nowhere. It learns them from the culture we feed it.

A.I. Toys uses machine learning to transform real toy-market data into absurd new toy concepts. The generated toys are funny because they are recognizable: their logic comes from actual product categories, marketing language, and social expectations.

Artist words

Gender is constructed through objects of play.

The toys described on Liu's screens were generated by a machine learning algorithm trained on real online consumer products labeled as "girls" and "boys" toys. Liu frames the resulting objects as reflections of the values adults place on children through design, marketing, and play.

Source: Liu, Ani. "A.I. Toys (MEGA HAUL RANDOM and MIXED SURPRISE ULTRA RARE UNBOXING COMPILATION!!!)." Ani Liu, 2022.

Research questions

Questions for the visitor

  • How do toys teach children which behaviors, futures, and social roles are expected of them?
  • What happens when AI systems learn from biased consumer datasets?
  • How does absurdity make cultural bias easier to see?
  • How do unboxing media, online shopping, and machine learning overlap?
03Visual and aesthetic representation

Visual and aesthetic representation

Images: Ani Liu. Source: ani-liu.com/ai-toys-screens.

Toy aesthetics

Liu adopts the aesthetics of toys, packaging, the retail shelf, and digital displays to immediately convey familiarity around the act of purchasing and unboxing toys. That familiarity is reinforced through bright colors and plastic materiality, then destabilized when the viewer sees the exaggerated blobby forms.

Visual analysis

Consumer culture becomes a database interface. The work borrows from product listings, toy packaging, unboxing videos, and screen-based browsing. A minimal Webcore structure lets the page echo online catalogs and early web indexes without burying the critique in visual noise.

04Methodology underlying the practice

A dataset trained on culture, then asked to speak it back

The project trains a machine-learning system on real, scraped online toy-product data, then prompts or conditions that system to generate new toys from the patterns it learned. When read carefully, the computational process is much less a neutral creative tool than an analytic instrument, it extracts latent cultural patterns from retail data and returns them in exaggerated form to the viewer.

Liu has also credited a group of students she was mentoring at Princeton with building the project alongside her, John Ahloy from the Math Department, Ryan Thorpe from Mechanical Engineering, and then very briefly Anoushka Mariwala, an Architecture student, so it can be read as an interdisciplinary approach to art.

Data provenance

The dataset comes from real consumer products sold on shopping sites such as Target and Amazon. That matters because the project's "bias" doesn't originate inside the algorithm alone, it originates across a longer chain:

manufacturer → marketing copy → retail taxonomy → search interface → scraped dataset → model training → generated toy

The model isn't inventing gender ideology from nothing. It's recomposing a market archive and a site for analysis. The full chain, extended with feedback and the people it circles back to, is diagrammed in the Actor-network diagram.

Material translation

The project doesn't stop at text generation, it translates outputs into screens, electronics, 3D printed toys, and display cases depending on the version. The 2021 A.I. Toys (Unboxing Mania) includes 3D printed toys, holographic vinyl, and a custom collector's case; the 2022 screen-based version includes screens, electronics, and 3D printed toys. Liu makes the algorithmic output feel like a consumer product, borrowing the language of retail display, toy packaging, and unboxing culture, then turning that language against itself.

Theoretical methods
Actor-network theory
Gender is not located in one actor alone. It emerges through an assemblage of platforms, parents, children, retailers, interfaces, toys, colors, copywriting, data labels, algorithms, and galleries.
Feminist technology studies
Technology is culturally situated, not outside society. AI is trained on society's residues.
Speculative / critical design
The generated toys aren't proposals for better toys. They're speculative artifacts that reveal the absurdity of existing norms.
Data critique
Datasets are not raw reality. They're structured by collection methods, commercial categories, and historical assumptions.
Material culture analysis
Toys are design objects that quietly train children into social roles.

06Rhetorical analysis

What the project is trying to say

The project argues that gender norms are not only taught through language or family structure, they're embedded in objects of play. Liu has described becoming interested in toys as a lens through which her daughter might form ideas about the world.

The claim underlying is: childhood objects are not innocent. A toy kitchen, doll, truck, weapon, pony, robot, or makeup kit is a small curriculum in itself.

"I started to see these toys as a lens by which she might form ideas about the world, and I became really interested in how we teach gender roles or norms through toys."

Ani Liu, Penn Q&A, 2021
Arguments
  1. 01

    Retail categories produce social categories.

    The "boys" and "girls" labels on retail platforms aren't passive descriptions. They help organize desire, expectation, and identity before the child even plays.

  2. 02

    AI reveals rather than escapes culture.

    The model's generated toys are absurd because the dataset is already absurd. The machine simply removes the polite veil.

  3. 03

    Design participates in social reproduction.

    Girls' toys skew toward domesticity, grooming, animals, and dolls; boys' toys skew toward violence, vehicles, electronics, construction, and superheroes. Liu describes this same contrast in her own project statement and in the Penn interview.

  4. 04

    Bias is infrastructural.

    The project doesn't blame one bad company, parent, toy, or algorithm. It shows a distributed system. Bias lives in the aisle, the menu, the product copy, the ad image, the search bar, the dataset, and the model.

How it's critical

The project is critical because it uses the very tools of contemporary technological culture, scraping, machine learning, product visualization, digital display, and 3D printing, to expose the assumptions inside that culture. It isn't anti-technology in a simple way; it asks what technologies learn from us, and what we then pretend they discovered independently.

There's a sharp rhetorical twist here too: the AI-generated toys look like hallucinations, but they're derived from real market logic. The satire lands because the absurdity isn't foreign, it's familiar. That's the little sting in the velvet box.

07Contemporaries

Contemporaries

Liu's work sits among artists and researchers examining machine vision, technological infrastructure, and the social life of designed objects. Tan Mu and Rachel Youn exhibited alongside Liu in the 2023 Offworlds group show at YveYANG Gallery; Kate Crawford and Trevor Paglen offer a close methodological parallel through their critique of training data.

Tan Mu

Silicon, 2021

Oil on linen. A painting of a purified silicon stone, the material that made the “Silicon Age” possible, the same hidden technical infrastructure that trains the machine-learning models behind A.I. Toys.

Rachel Youn

Pendulum, 2022

Dismantled baby swing, steel sawhorses, artificial plant, soil. A discarded children's object is stripped down and kept in motion, a physical echo of how A.I. Toys keeps gendered consumer objects moving through a machine.

Kate Crawford + Trevor Paglen

ImageNet Roulette, 2019

An interactive project trained on ImageNet's categories for people. By returning racist, misogynistic, absurd, and otherwise harmful labels, it made the politics embedded in training data visible. Like A.I. Toys, the work lets a classification system expose the cultural assumptions it has inherited.

08Contextual analysis

Contextual analysis

Mid-2010s

Algorithms are not neutral

The work builds on, and comments on, machine bias explored by journalists like Julia Angwin. By the mid-2010s, the idea that algorithms can reproduce racial, gender, economic, and other forms of bias had already been established and had begun moving into art, design, and media circles.

Parenthood

Gendered toys become personal evidence

From a personal standpoint, Liu has stated that the project grew out of becoming a parent and noticing the gendered nature of toys she was purchasing for her daughter.

2021–2022

Machine learning enters the art field

A.I. Toys was developed around 2021–2022, a recent moment but also one near the beginning of increased machine-learning use in art production. The work also arrives a few years after Target's initiative to stop separating "girls' toys" and "boys' toys" in its stores.

Ecologies of Care

Care work and childcare objects

The project is nested within Liu's Ecologies of Care exhibition, which examined postpartum experiences, the labor of motherhood, and the material cultures of childcare. The exhibition included programmed feeding tubes, transparent sculptures of a pig's uterus encasing human fetuses, and drawings depicting digital screen times.

09Fit within Liu's wider practice

Turning intimate life into a research site

A.I. Toys fits naturally into Liu's broader practice, which repeatedly examines the relationship between technology, embodiment, gender, reproduction, labor, and social systems. Her own site organizes work across biological, digital, and analog categories, motherhood, bodies, machine perception, affect, algorithmic systems.

Her hybrid method traces back to her training: Penn describes Liu as an interdisciplinary artist whose practice reflects graduate study at both the MIT Media Lab and the Harvard Graduate School of Design, exploring how new technologies affect perception, information, and identity.

A.I. Toys also sits close to the concerns of Ecologies of Care, the exhibition made during Liu's postpartum period. Within that context, the project isn't only about toy marketing, it belongs to a broader inquiry into how care, reproduction, gender, and technology are organized by systems that appear ordinary.

Liu repeatedly turns intimate life into a research site. The body, the child, the toy, the pump, the dataset, and the algorithm become instruments for measuring cultural values usually treated as private, natural, or inevitable.

Sources: "Q&A: Ani Liu, Professor of Practice in Fine Arts." University of Pennsylvania, Stuart Weitzman School of Design, 14 Oct. 2021. Artishock. "ECOLOGIES of CARE." Artishock Revista, 2 Aug. 2022.

11Personal assessment

Appraisal and criticism

The sections above analyze the project; this one states a position on it, in the author's own voice.

Appraisal

A.I. Toys is strong because its method is unusually legible. The viewer doesn't need to understand machine learning technically to grasp the critique: take gendered toy data, train a model, generate new toys, let the outputs indict the inputs. It's a clean loop.

The project is also effective because it resists the common trap of treating AI as a mystical oracle. Liu makes AI boring in the best possible way, a pattern machine, a clerk of cultural habits. The horror isn't that the machine is alien. The horror is that it's obedient.

Its best quality is that it makes classification visible as design. The dropdown menu, the product tag, the color palette, and the marketing phrase become actors, which, for a computational-design precedent, is especially useful: it shows how data structures are already spatial, social, and ideological.

Criticism

The main limitation is methodological opacity. Public descriptions confirm scraped toy data and a machine-learning model, but not dataset size, sampling method, model architecture, training process, or evaluation criteria. That opacity doesn't weaken the work's conceptual force, but it limits how rigorously the computational method itself can be assessed.

A second limitation: the project relies on a binary, "boys" toys versus "girls" toys, to critique that binary. Probably necessary, since the retail platforms themselves impose the structure, but there's a risk the binary stays visually and organizationally dominant even while being questioned.

A third: the generated toys risk becoming too entertaining. Their absurdity carries real rhetorical force, but a viewer could consume them as funny AI weirdness rather than a critique of gendered social reproduction.

Final assessment

As a precedent for computational design, A.I. Toys is valuable because it demonstrates that machine learning can be used not only to optimize, automate, or produce novelty, but to diagnose a cultural system. Its contribution is methodological as much as aesthetic: it turns a dataset into a mirror, a toy into an argument, and a retail category into evidence.
